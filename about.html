oh, i'm also a nikon shooter

More specifically, I use the Nikon D810. Absolute beast of a camera. Fucking love that thing to death. Although it's around eight years old at this point, much of what it's capable of still holds up amazingly to this day. In fact, I think that the image quality you'd be able to get out of this thing even trumps modern professional cameras. The only thing that I don't like about it is that it lacks any sort of articulating screen, and that it doesn't repsond to touch. Shooting low to the ground is a gamble most of the time since I can't even see what I'm shooting at in live view so I'm just spraying and praying. Speaking of live view... oh my god the autofocus in live view is absolute garbage. Oh? You wanna focus on that object that is only slightly out of focus? Great, I'll hunt the entire range of what I can focus on and maybe hit what you're looking for. Oh? There was a spec of dust that came into frame while I'm already focused on something? Of course I can start hunting for you! Oh? There is absolutely notHING GOING ON??? YEAH, GREAT, I'LL JUST START FUCKING HUNTING BECAUSE THAT'S WHAT I DO BEST.


i have more than one lens

So, you wanna know my lens collection? I guess I'll try to keep this one more to the point. In short, I have:

  • NIKKOR 50mm f/1.8
    • The old reliable. The nifty fifty. Sharp, nimble, and delivers that scrumptious bokeh.
  • Rokinon 85mm f/1.4

  • Tamron 28-300mm f/3.5-6.3
    • Yes, the superzoom with optical stabilization. Jack of all trades. Not much else to really say about this lens, though. Just great if you only wanna take one lens with you and you have no idea what you wanna shoot.
  • Tamron 19-35mm f/3.5-4.5
    • So I was originally planning on buying the 14-24mm f/2.8 for $720 at a clearance sale, but I found this guy instead. Don't get me wrong, $720 for that lens is a steal, but this Tamron was only $175! $175!! Full frame as well! I don't even know why this lens is valued so low because this thing is great. Gets super wide with the ability to punch in a little, makes a super loud and adorable sound when it focuses since the motors turn the actual focus ring, can even fit a filter at this focal range! Sure, it's not as fast as the f/2.8, but do you really need a fast lens if you're shooting wide? Nikkormat FTn

I was shopping around for an F3HP or an F2, but they were all well above what I could reasonably afford, espeically since I had tuition to pay. Then, I found this gem for only $50!!! I mean, I looked on eBay and other sites and that is about what is camera this is worth these days but it's still an amazing little guy. Sure, it doesn't have any sort of shoe mount, only goes up to 1/1000s, and has a light meter that only works with 1.35V batteries that you can no longer find today, but shooting with this camera is still a pleasure.

Now, I absolutely do not know a lot of what I'm talking about since I am very new to analog photography but as long as I am having a good time with it, that's all the matters right?


A very, very handsome camera.
You can see the Peak Design plate I put on the base.
This particular Nikkormat features those little rabbit ears on the mount ring. To be honest, I have no idea what are they used for and what it meands in terms of lens compatibility.
Disappointed there was no film tab holder. Not because I'll forget what I put in there, but I just think it looks cool.
The viewfinder is one of the biggest and brightest ones I have ever tried. Easily beats out my D810, even though it's roughly 60 years older.
The camera looks a bit ridiculous with my Tamron sitting on it considering it's an AF lens, and doesn't match the aesthetic at all.
It's a look that's grown on me.

One thing I love about this camera is that it's entirely mechanical. Unlike the F3, everything will still fucntion (imaging wise) with or without a battery. It makes me apprecitae that what I'm shooting with is all powered by its inner workings of whateverthefuck designed by engineering geniuses, rather than just a mundane electric circuit.

Of course, like I said, the only thing that does need the battery is the light meter. And since you have to use 1.35V batteries which are not manufactured anymore it'll be pretty rare to have the opportunity to consistently shoot with a working meter.

However, any self-respecting film photographer will probably use their own intuition to accurately meter the exposure themselves, rather than rely on a tool. I guess that's what I'm currently doing when I do go out and shoot with this camera, but since I have never had the need to think about what settings to use when shooting (thanks D810 aperture priority) my judgement is still very rudimentary. I've just been using the sunny 16 rule, and making the recommended adjustments under different lighting conditions to guess my exposure.

Also, to set shutter speed you have to turn the ring around the mount. I'll admit, it's a pretty neat spot but I think I would prefer to just have the controls on the top like most other caneras. Also, with how much hardware you're moving it can get a litlte tough to push adjust it.

Simple, elegant, stylish. Also really grimey.
The back is minimal yet gives you just enough of the information you need. Love the little window.
Although the "L35AF" branding is a bit corny, it's tasteful in my opinion.
Interestingly, you can see the outline of where the quality control sticker used to be. I mean, it's kind of fitting.
This was probably the first camera Nikon made with the power switch dial on the shutter button. They've been using that design ever since, along with some other camera manufacturers.
The flash pops automatically whenever the light meter detects that there's low light. It's cute.
So the mounting points for the strap on this camera is actually on the side. Allows the body to hang down vertically when wearing the strap cross-body.
The bottom features a two-step film rewind button so you don't accidentally trigger it.
One thing that drew me to this camera was the fact that it took your standard AA batteries!! No need to source some sort of obscure obselete batteries.
It's a shame that it's not in a functional state :c

Fujica 35 ML

Calm down, yes, this isn't a Nikon camera. However, this was the first film camera I decided to pick up since it was only $20 on Kijiji. Now I know why you don't just buy film cameras for $20 and expect to salvage it, let it have it function. The aperture blades are stuck, and the shutter won't even open if I try to take a shot. Took it to Downtown Camera to get it looked at and get this- a $250 quote to get this thing maybe working sicne it's such an old niche camera. But hey, it makes for a very stylish paperweight.


Also a very, very handsome looking boy.
Interestingly, the film advance lever is at the bottom. The guy I had look at it called it "silly."
THe inside is mint. A stark contrast to the worn, corroded, fungal ouside.
Another quirk is that in order to focus, you must spin a dial on the back. There's also these unusual focus markings for different "openings."
One cool thing about this camera is that you can couple the aperture and shutter speed controls so that the overall exposure stays the same.

This thing is built like a tank, though. the build quality is astonishing and feels way heavier than it looks. It's almost as heavy as my D810 without a lens. They really didn't mess around with film camera construction back in the day. So yeah, now it's retired to being a paperweight.
